Book Review

Genesis Alpha, by Rune Michaels, is a thriller that to many would be a riveting book. Josh embodies the typical teenager who is addicted to a video game, and has no life outside what he is doing within the game. This is very relatable, good choice for a main character, as many teens have similar feelings about video games. However, his life takes a turn from a typical teenager's everyday experiences when his brother is blamed for the murder of a girl named Karen, a brother that when Josh was younger was born to help save from cancer. Max's reaction is very typical of somebody in his situation - of sheer shock and utter belief in Max's innocence. As the story progresses, however, Rachel, the sister of the girl Max allegedly murders, gives Josh proof and helps turn allegedly into probably. However, because of his "gift" to Josh earlier in life, he's not sure if he is at all responsible - after all, part of Josh is inside of Max!

Max, at first, seemed like a nice, responsible young man that was wrongly accused of a crime he didn't commit, but as the story progresses his true feelings are shown, which makes the reader question their previous judgement. During their second meeting, Max happens to make a comment that leaves Josh wondering what exactly he meant. This comment slowly leads to Max's discovery of a big secret, one that his parents had been keeping since his birth. Max and Josh are clones - as his mom puts it, they are just "twins born a few years apart, with some scientific help. That's all.", as Max puts it, he's Josh's reserve parts, if he were to become sick again. Issues like this make me wonder whether these kind of discussions will happen within my lifetime, and if "designer babies" will become the norm.

At the start of the book, Rachel, the sister of Karen, shows her anger towards Max through Josh. At first, her tale is of a chilling person that Josh could never believe to be Max. As the story unfolds, Josh learns that he didn't really know Max as well as he thought. As many believe there to be a connection between the two brothers, because of Josh's donation to Max. Rachel's extortion and punishment of Josh, on Max's behalf, made me not want to side with her in the first place. But, as her evil lashing-out for her sister's death to somebody who really did nothing continued, her actions made me despise her. Rachel was one of the most evil characters I have ever read in a book - her actions toward Josh made me mad and forced me, more than once, to put down the book. However, as the story progresses, her evil becomes less intense and by the end the tense relationship has resolved itself. All in all, readers end up realizing that Rachel isn't quite as evil as was though from the beginning, and that her brutality was, though directed at the wrong person, justified.

Though I did finish the book, knowing what I know now, I wouldn't read it again. The tension between Rachel and Josh near the start of the book made me wish that Rachel would just go back to where she came from. As the tension between the two characters climaxed and started to resolve, I was relieved. This book helps show that good and evil are both relative terms and the perception of a person's good or evil can change in a split-second.

Theme Analysis

"Attack the evil that is within yourself, rather than attacking the evil that is in others."
Confucius

Throughout Genesis Alpha, a common theme is that without proper outlets for anger, it can intensify and cause issues later in life. If both Max and Rachel decided to address their internal feelings instead of lashing out on other members of society, the whole conflict could have been avoided. Max, instead of talking with his parents or another trusted friend about his feelings of abandonment as a child after Josh's birth, decides to respond with anger and resentment toward others. Because now their parents are addressing the issue for the first time, Max isn't really open to the discussion and cuts them out of his life - he sees it as too little too late on their part. Rachel, instead of looking for healthy ways to deal with her anger, takes it out on Josh. She blames him for everything Max did to her sister even though he has no control over Max's actions. This story illustrated that people lashing out at others is not an effective way to deal with your problems.

What is assumed at this point is that Max's rage caused Karen's murder. Assuming that this scenario is correct, he could have avoided this in may ways. Perhaps his parents or brother could have noticed his festering emotions earlier and could have gotten him help to avoid this tragedy. Max's increasingly hurtful comments show that he isn't willing to take responsibility for his own actions. Instead, he lashes out at others, blaming them for what he should really blame himself for. While Max and Rachel project their guilt and insecurities on others, what is really necessary is to internalize this blame and to be accountable for their actions.

Character Analysis

  • Josh Seville Josh's actions were just in telling his dad about Max's character Rook, but he really should have mentioned Rachel earlier. Josh, at the start, is motivated by his belief that Max is innocent, but as the story progresses he becomes driven to discover the truth about his birth and why he was created in the first place. Though I may end up doing something similar if I were actually in Josh's position, I would hope that my anger wouldn't get the best of me when dealing with somebody as messed up as Rachel.
  • Max Seville While I don't really know why Max murdered Karen, something wasn't quite right in Max's reasoning - whether he didn't really realize what he was doing or was just insane. Max is, at first, described as being a nice person who seems perfectly legitimate in motives and desires. As the story progresses, we get to see how Max converts from this reasonable person, to someone who his own mother can barely stand. By the end, Max has changed to such a degree that he decides to throw his family under the bus just to decrease his prison sentence. That is a move that, in the end, shows how much he has changed from before he went to college to after and how he feels about his family then and now.
  • Parents of Josh and Max Even though I cannot speak from personal experience, both parent seem to over-react at every new little detail that is dug up by the media. They really should have slowed down and taken each bit of information slowly and methodically. Some of the pain was inflicted by their past actions, like their hiding the fact Josh was a clone from him for such a long time, but other things, such as Max's betrayal, were out of their control and they shouldn't take so harshly upon themselves.
  • Rachel Rachel, contrary to what an average reader may think, was trying to act rationally. She was trying to get revenge for what Max did to her sister in the only way she could think how - by hurting a "seperate part" of Max. While in the end she discovers that Josh and Max are quite separate people (Josh can feel pain/angst toward others while Max doesn't) she took her rational feelings of revenge a little too far in trying to blame Josh for something in which he really had no part in whatsoever.
